# Glossary Definition Candidates

Terms found in *Gaming the Great Plague* that appear in user-facing game text and may need glossary definitions for a non-specialist (middle school) audience. Terms that already have definition widgets are excluded.

---

## 1. Plague-Specific Roles and Practices

### Searcher / Searcher of the Dead
- **Context:** One of the plague work roles the player can be assigned. "Searchers of the dead (often elderly women) could be bribed to record a different cause of death."
- **Why it needs a definition:** A specialized 17th-century role — women who examined corpses to determine cause of death. No modern equivalent exists.

### Corpse Bearer / Corpsebearer
- **Context:** A plague work role appearing in monthly passages (July–September 1665) and image captions. "A corpse bearer; reproduction of an engraving from a series of London Cries, 1655."
- **Why it needs a definition:** The person whose job it was to collect dead bodies during the plague. Unfamiliar to modern readers.

### Warder
- **Context:** One of the plague work roles — "stand guard outside quarantined houses to prevent anyone from escaping."
- **Why it needs a definition:** A plague-era guard assigned to watch quarantined houses. Different from the modern use of "warder" (prison guard).

### Nurse (in plague context)
- **Context:** "Pay an old woman to nurse you"; plague nurses who cared for the sick in quarantined homes.
- **Why it needs a definition:** Not a modern trained nurse; these were often poor women who cared for plague patients, sometimes accused of theft or hastening death.

### Plague Pit
- **Context:** "Put into the communal plague pits, late at night with no one to witness your burial."
- **Why it needs a definition:** Mass graves for plague dead. While somewhat self-explanatory, the scale and practice may need context.

### Dead Cart
- **Context:** Depicted in images of corpse bearers loading plague victims onto carts.
- **Why it needs a definition:** Carts used to collect corpses from houses during plague outbreaks, often at night.

### Shut Up / Shutting Up (of houses)
- **Context:** "Your neighbors shut up the entrances to your house and paint a red cross." "Houses are shut up with red crosses."
- **Why it needs a definition:** The practice of sealing plague-infected houses with all inhabitants inside. The phrase "shut up" in this context is very different from its modern slang meaning.

### Red Cross (on doors)
- **Context:** "Paint a red cross with the words Lord Have Mercy on the front door."
- **Why it needs a definition:** The official marking placed on plague houses to warn others. Young readers may not know this practice.

### Lord Have Mercy
- **Context:** "Red crosses and the phrase Lord Have Mercy Upon Us have been painted on their front doors."
- **Why it needs a definition:** While the phrase is somewhat self-explanatory, its specific use as a plague-house inscription is historically specific.

## 2. Medical and Scientific Terms (Period)

### Mithridatium
- **Context:** Appears in the defBlisteringPlaster definition — "This plaster was usually made of either Mithridatium or Galene."
- **Why it needs a definition:** An ancient compound medicine/antidote named after King Mithridates VI. Obscure even for adults.

### Galene
- **Context:** Appears in the defBlisteringPlaster definition — "This plaster was usually made of either Mithridatium or Galene."
- **Why it needs a definition:** A type of medical preparation associated with Galen. Very obscure.

### Consumption
- **Context:** "Consumption and Tissick: 4,808 [deaths]" — listed as the top non-plague cause of death in the final stats.
- **Why it needs a definition:** The historical name for tuberculosis. Not commonly known by middle schoolers.

### Tissick / Tisick
- **Context:** "Consumption and Tissick" listed as a cause of death in the final stats.
- **Why it needs a definition:** An archaic term for a chronic cough or pulmonary disease. Completely unfamiliar to modern readers.

### Dropsy
- **Context:** "Dropsy and Tympany: 1,478 [deaths]" in the final stats.
- **Why it needs a definition:** The historical term for edema (fluid retention/swelling). Archaic.

### Tympany
- **Context:** "Dropsy and Tympany" in the final stats.
- **Why it needs a definition:** Abdominal swelling/distension. Very archaic medical term.

### Surfeit
- **Context:** "Surfeit deaths were likely misattributed plague cases" in the final stats.
- **Why it needs a definition:** Historically meant illness from overindulgence in food or drink. Used as a cause of death in bills of mortality.

### Spotted Fever
- **Context:** Listed as a cause of death in infection-widgets data.
- **Why it needs a definition:** Historical name for typhus or other fevers with skin spots. Unfamiliar to modern readers.

### Stopping of the Stomach
- **Context:** Listed as a historical cause of death in infection-widgets data.
- **Why it needs a definition:** Archaic diagnosis for digestive/stomach ailments. Meaningless to modern readers without context.

### Griping in the Guts
- **Context:** Listed as a historical cause of death in infection-widgets data.
- **Why it needs a definition:** Severe abdominal pain, likely from dysentery or intestinal infections. Colorful but unfamiliar period terminology.

### Chrisomes and Infants
- **Context:** "Chrisomes and Infants (infant deaths) was the second-largest cause of death" in the final stats.
- **Why it needs a definition:** A chrisom child was a baby who died within a month of baptism (still wearing its christening cloth). Very obscure.

### Childbed
- **Context:** "Childbed (maternal mortality) killed 625 women in 1665" in the final stats.
- **Why it needs a definition:** Death in childbirth. The term "childbed" is archaic.

### Teeth and Worms
- **Context:** "Teeth and Worms (infant deaths): 2,614" in the final stats.
- **Why it needs a definition:** Historical cause-of-death category for infant deaths attributed to teething or parasitic worms. Misleading to modern readers.

### Rising of the Lights
- **Context:** Listed as a cause of death in infection-widgets / bills of mortality data.
- **Why it needs a definition:** An archaic diagnosis referring to a lung condition (the "lights" were lungs). Completely opaque to modern readers.

### King's Evil
- **Context:** Listed as a cause of death in infection-widgets data.
- **Why it needs a definition:** Scrofula (tuberculous cervical lymphadenitis); believed curable by the touch of the king. Very archaic.

### Overlaid and Starved
- **Context:** "Overlaid and Starved (45 — mostly infants accidentally smothered)" in the final stats.
- **Why it needs a definition:** "Overlaid" meant an infant smothered while sleeping with a parent. Not intuitive for modern readers.

### Yersinia pestis
- **Context:** Appears in the defPlague definition — "a deadly infection usually spread to humans by fleas infected with the bacterium yersinia pestis."
- **Why it needs a definition:** The scientific name of the plague bacterium. Some readers may not know Latin scientific nomenclature.

### Pneumonic (plague)
- **Context:** defPlague definition — "including bubonic (lymph node), pneumonic (lungs), and septicemic (bloodstream) plague."
- **Why it needs a definition:** A form of plague affecting the lungs. Medical terminology that may be unfamiliar.

### Septicemic (plague)
- **Context:** defPlague definition — "septicemic (bloodstream) plague… was 100% fatal."
- **Why it needs a definition:** A form of plague affecting the bloodstream. Technical medical terminology.

## 3. Government, Law, and Parish Administration

### Churchwarden
- **Context:** "Your churchwardens take mercy on you and don't fine you for skipping"; "your churchwardens know that you only stay away because you fear contagion."
- **Why it needs a definition:** An elected parish officer responsible for church property and enforcing attendance. Not a modern concept for most Americans.

### Vestry
- **Context:** "The vestry of $parish has called you to serve"; "the vestry of $parish decided to remove you from your position."
- **Why it needs a definition:** The governing body of a parish, made up of local property owners. An unfamiliar English church/government concept.

### Parish Clerk
- **Context:** "An official known as the parish clerk kept local records of christenings, burials, and marriages."
- **Why it needs a definition:** The local record-keeper for a parish. While self-explanatory in context, the role and significance may not be clear.

### Debtor's Prison
- **Context:** "Your creditors have demanded you be thrown in debtor's prison."
- **Why it needs a definition:** A prison where people were confined until they paid their debts. An abolished practice that may confuse modern readers.

### The Fleet (prison)
- **Context:** "You are committed to the Fleet"; "you go to the Fleet prison where you find a priest willing to marry you in a nearby tavern."
- **Why it needs a definition:** A specific London prison for debtors. Could be confused with "the fleet" (navy). Already has a definition widget for the naval fleet, but the prison use may need clarification.

### Naval Board
- **Context:** "Argue your case in front of the Naval Board" in the impressed/navy-volunteer passages.
- **Why it needs a definition:** The administrative body governing the Royal Navy. An institution unfamiliar to modern readers.

### Curfew (plague context)
- **Context:** "The city has imposed a 9pm curfew for healthy people, so that the sick can go out for a bit of air."
- **Why it needs a definition:** The inverted curfew — where healthy people stayed indoors so sick people could get air — is counter-intuitive and historically specific.

## 4. Social and Economic Terms

### Deserving Poor
- **Context:** "You are one of the deserving poor and have a license to beg in your parish."
- **Why it needs a definition:** A legal/social distinction between beggars who were settled in a parish (licensed) and itinerant/vagrant beggars. A key concept of early modern English poor law.

### Man of Business
- **Context:** "You instruct your man of business to make sure none of your tenants are illegally subletting."
- **Why it needs a definition:** A personal financial agent or estate manager for wealthy people. Unfamiliar term.

### Courtier
- **Context:** "Court is abuzz with gossip about the war and there's plenty to keep an ambitious courtier busy."
- **Why it needs a definition:** A person who attends the royal court to gain favor. The concept of court politics may need explanation for younger readers.

### Head of Household
- **Context:** "If there is an adult male living in the home, he is generally considered the head of the household."
- **Why it needs a definition:** While somewhat familiar, the legal/social implications in the 17th century (control over finances, legal standing) differ significantly from modern understanding.

### Apprentice / Journeyman
- **Context:** "Households may additionally include apprentices, journeymen, and servants."
- **Why it needs a definition:** Stages in the guild/craft system. An apprentice was learning a trade; a journeyman had completed training but didn't yet own a shop. This system is defunct.

### Guild
- **Context:** "They were usually members of a guild" in the defArtisan definition.
- **Why it needs a definition:** A medieval/early modern professional association. While taught in some schools, the specifics of how guilds operated may be unclear.

### Cordwainer
- **Context:** Listed as a type of artisan trade alongside grocers, tailors, and butchers.
- **Why it needs a definition:** A shoemaker who works with new leather. Completely unfamiliar to modern readers.

### Chandler
- **Context:** One of the artisan trades the player can be assigned.
- **Why it needs a definition:** A maker/seller of candles (or, more broadly, a general provisions dealer). Not a familiar term.

### Broadside (printed)
- **Context:** "You can subscribe to the weekly Bills of Mortality, printed broadsides."
- **Why it needs a definition:** A large single-sided printed sheet. Different from a naval "broadside." The concept of early printed news may need explanation.

### Pounds, Shillings, and Pence (currency)
- **Context:** Throughout the game in money displays. Uses symbols like "s." and "d."
- **Why it needs a definition:** Pre-decimal English currency. The "d." for pence (from Latin *denarius*) and "s." for shillings (from Latin *solidus*), and the conversion (12 pence = 1 shilling, 20 shillings = 1 pound) would be opaque to most American students.

## 5. Religious Terms

### Communion / The Sacrament
- **Context:** "You are reported for skipping communion and fined"; "can't bring yourself to join in their heretical communion."
- **Why it needs a definition:** The Christian ritual of bread and wine. While widely known, its legal obligation in 17th-century England (skipping could result in fines) may need context.

### Heretical / Heresy
- **Context:** "Can't bring yourself to join in their heretical communion"; "You can't bear to even set foot in a heretical church."
- **Why it needs a definition:** Beliefs officially condemned by the church. The concept of one Christian denomination viewing another as heretical may need explanation for younger readers.

### Presbyteries / Presbytery
- **Context:** "Protestants who wanted their church to be ruled by presbyteries, or a collection of elders and ministers."
- **Why it needs a definition:** A governing body of church elders. Ecclesiastical governance terminology that is obscure.

### Anglican
- **Context:** "Most commonly Anglicans (Church of England)" in defCatholic and defDissident definitions.
- **Why it needs a definition:** A member of the Church of England. While taught in some history classes, the term may be unfamiliar to some middle schoolers.

### Conventicle
- **Context:** The concept of illegal religious meetings of dissenters is depicted in the game (e.g., Presbyterians arrested at an illegal meeting in Covent Garden).
- **Why it needs a definition:** An illegal religious gathering of dissenters. Very specific historical term.

## 6. Historical Places and Institutions

### Pudding Lane
- **Context:** "The fire began in the King's Baker's house on Pudding Lane" (September 1666).
- **Why it needs a definition:** The street where the Great Fire of London started. Historically significant but not self-explanatory for American students.

### Moorfields
- **Context:** "The pest house and plague pit, Moorfields, London" in image captions.
- **Why it needs a definition:** An open area north of the City walls used for plague burial pits. Needs geographic context.

### Bedlam / Bethlem Burial Ground
- **Context:** "Commonly known as Bedlam or Bethlem burial ground because it was located in the precinct of the Priory of St. Mary of Bethlehem."
- **Why it needs a definition:** While "bedlam" is a common English word meaning chaos, its origin as a London hospital/burial ground may interest readers.

### Tothill Fields Bridewell
- **Context:** "The prison known as Tothill Fields Bridewell, which operated between 1618 and 1884."
- **Why it needs a definition:** A specific London prison. "Bridewell" was a generic term for a house of correction/workhouse. Young readers would not know this.

### Bridewell
- **Context:** "Bridewell Precinct" appears as a parish/location; "Tothill Fields Bridewell" as a prison.
- **Why it needs a definition:** A house of correction or workhouse. The term became generic for such institutions. Unfamiliar to modern readers.

### Declaration of Breda
- **Context:** "The exiled Charles II issued the Declaration of Breda, in which he promised to pardon…"
- **Why it needs a definition:** The 1660 declaration that paved the way for the restoration of the monarchy. Important historical context.

## 7. Historical People and Events

### Charles II / King Charles II
- **Context:** "The merry monarch King Charles II was restored to the thrones of England, Scotland, and Ireland." Appears in multiple passages throughout the game.
- **Why it needs a definition:** While mentioned frequently, the player may not know who he was or why he was called the "merry monarch."

### Merry Monarch
- **Context:** "The merry monarch King Charles II" in the start and identity passages.
- **Why it needs a definition:** A nickname for Charles II reflecting his court's atmosphere after the austere Commonwealth period. The epithet needs context.

### Battle of Lowestoft
- **Context:** "The English fleet wins a great victory over the Dutch at the Battle of Lowestoft."
- **Why it needs a definition:** A major naval battle of the Second Anglo-Dutch War. Unfamiliar to most American students.

### Four Days Battle
- **Context:** "The fleet engages the Dutch again. Four days of cannon fire are heard from London."
- **Why it needs a definition:** A major 1666 naval engagement. Unfamiliar to most readers.

### St. James's Day Battle
- **Context:** "The St. James' Day Battle on August 4th, 1666 between English and Dutch ships."
- **Why it needs a definition:** Another Anglo-Dutch War naval battle. Obscure for most readers.

### Oliver Cromwell / Lord Protector
- **Context:** "The installation of Oliver Cromwell as the Lord Protector of England" in defCommonwealth and defCivilWar.
- **Why it needs a definition:** Partially covered by existing definitions, but "Lord Protector" as a title may need its own entry since it appears in user-facing text.

### Gregory King (statistician)
- **Context:** "In 1688, statistician Gregory King estimated…" in defNoble, defArtisan, defMerchant definitions.
- **Why it needs a definition:** Referenced as a source for household numbers. Readers may wonder who he was.

### HMS Royal Sovereign
- **Context:** "You have enlisted on HMS Royal Sovereign" in December 1664 and navy-volunteer passages.
- **Why it needs a definition:** A specific warship. "HMS" (His/Her Majesty's Ship) may need explanation for younger readers.

## 8. Military and Naval Terms

### Impressment
- **Context:** "There is no escape from your impressment" in the impressed passage.
- **Why it needs a definition:** The practice of forced naval conscription. The noun form appears alongside the already-defined "impressed" but may need additional standalone context.

## 9. Domestic and Daily Life Terms

### Hearth
- **Context:** "A fire in your hearth"; "a fire in each of your many hearths."
- **Why it needs a definition:** A fireplace/firepit. While familiar to some, younger readers in homes without fireplaces may not know the term.

### Alehouse
- **Context:** "Spend as much of your — admittedly little — free time in the streets and alehouses."
- **Why it needs a definition:** A tavern or pub that primarily served ale. Distinct from an inn or coffeehouse. Period-specific.

### Tavern
- **Context:** "A priest willing to marry you in a nearby tavern"; "a market, taverns, and sex workers moved into the south side."
- **Why it needs a definition:** While more familiar than "alehouse," the concept of taverns as social centers and impromptu wedding venues is period-specific.

### Hovel
- **Context:** "Lodgings that are practically a hovel and well beneath your station."
- **Why it needs a definition:** A small, poorly built dwelling. Some younger readers may not know this word.

### Station (social)
- **Context:** "Well beneath your station"; "beneath your social station."
- **Why it needs a definition:** One's social rank or class position in society. The concept of fixed social hierarchy may need explanation.

## 10. Bills of Mortality and Record-Keeping Terms

### Christenings
- **Context:** "Parish clerk kept local records of christenings, burials, and marriages."
- **Why it needs a definition:** A baptism ceremony, especially in the Church of England. Some readers may not know this term.

### Banns
- **Context:** "The priest reads the banns and then you are married"; "Rather than wait for three weeks to have the banns read."
- **Why it needs a definition:** A public announcement of an intended marriage, read in church on three successive Sundays. An unfamiliar practice for most modern Americans.

## 11. Fire-Related Terms

### Conflagration
- **Context:** "You stumble through the streets to flee the conflagration" (September 1666).
- **Why it needs a definition:** A large destructive fire. A vocabulary word that middle schoolers may not know.

### King's Baker
- **Context:** "The fire began in the King's Baker's house on Pudding Lane."
- **Why it needs a definition:** The royal baker Thomas Farriner, in whose bakery the Great Fire of London started. The concept of a "King's Baker" as a royal appointment needs explanation.

## 12. Legal and Social Practices

### Transported / Transportation
- **Context:** "The local authorities decide to have you transported to the New World"; "shipped to the colony of Virginia."
- **Why it needs a definition:** Forced deportation to the colonies as punishment. A major historical practice unfamiliar to many readers.

### Colony of Virginia
- **Context:** "Bound into indentured servitude and shipped to the colony of Virginia."
- **Why it needs a definition:** While Virginia is familiar, its status as a colony and destination for transported convicts/laborers in the 1660s may need context.

### License to Beg
- **Context:** "You are one of the deserving poor and have a license to beg in your parish."
- **Why it needs a definition:** Formal legal permission to beg within one's own parish. The concept that begging was regulated and licensed is surprising to modern readers.

### Fined for Non-Attendance (at church)
- **Context:** "You are reported for skipping communion and fined"; "don't fine you for non-attendance."
- **Why it needs a definition:** It was illegal to skip Church of England services. Mandatory church attendance enforced by fines is unfamiliar to modern readers.

## 13. Reputation System Labels

### "A worthy soul" / "Of good credit and quality" / "Bad credit is better than none" / "A worthless and base rogue"
- **Context:** Used as reputation tier labels in the stats/decision-history passage.
- **Why they need definitions:** These period-appropriate phrases use "credit" in its archaic sense (social reputation/trustworthiness rather than financial credit). "Base" meaning low/dishonorable is also archaic.

## 14. Other Notable Terms

### Ward (orphan context)
- **Context:** Orphaned children become "wards" of a guardian.
- **Why it needs a definition:** A person (usually a child) under the legal guardianship of someone other than their parent. While still used legally, the term may be unfamiliar to middle schoolers.

### Esquire
- **Context:** "John Belson, Esquire" in the defStGilesPowder definition.
- **Why it needs a definition:** A title of social rank below a knight. While still used in addresses (Esq.), its meaning is unclear to most.

### "Send not to know for whom the church bells toll"
- **Context:** Opening line of the death passage.
- **Why it needs a definition:** A paraphrase of John Donne's famous meditation. Younger readers may not recognize the allusion or understand the archaic phrasing.

### Comet (as portent)
- **Context:** Comets seen as divine portents of plague/disaster in December 1664.
- **Why it needs a definition:** The belief that comets predicted plague was widespread in 1665 London. This differs from modern scientific understanding of comets.

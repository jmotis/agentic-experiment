When the authors crafted a detailed story line, I found myself enthralled. Their storytelling was illustrative of historians’ ability to show how narratives likely first capture their attention. Still, the sheer size of this work is daunting. The breadth of sources creates a “good problem” in that their evidence is overwhelming (xxxii). Their source base demonstrates the authors’ credibility and to what extent seemingly disparate finds can relate to one another.

In terms of its historiographical addition, I found it refreshing that the authors pushed against the norm — Atlantic World — and chose to highlight England and its European connections (xxxi). (I say this as someone who would like to explore Britain from the Atlantic World perspective.) Their approach to trace ideological exchanges draws upon a traditional methodology, but I had yet to encounter a study of how written rumors circulated (xxxiii). In a current era of “fake news,” this examination made me realize to what extent our modern day “phenomenon” possesses quite a history. I would be curious to see whether other rumors with similar attention affected other periods of disruption such as the Age of Revolutions.

]]> By: Edward Kirsch https://2022hist635.jessicaotis.com/2022/03/21/the-murder-of-king-james-i-book-review/#comment-62 Thu, 24 Mar 2022 00:13:41 +0000 https://2022hist635.jessicaotis.com/?p=583#comment-62 I concur wholeheartedly with Callan’s assessment that “Cogswell and Ballamy . . . include copious amounts of detail that distract from the arguments made.” While reading, I had the sense that the authors were determined to quote, nearly in full, and discuss in excruciating detail, every pamphlet, poem, letter, or other evidence that mentioned, referenced, or arguably could be shown to have some connection, no matter how remote or tenuous, to their “Secret History”! (dramatic pause)
The book would have benefited greatly from some judicious selection of the most relevant supporting evidence (and opposed evidence). Reading the book, I was reminded why judges in federal court impose page limitations on briefs. They expect the attorneys to select the most relevant facts and I expect no less of historians. (There is a Navy adage that I will modify for present purposes: “If everything is a priority (important), then nothing is a priority (important).” Just in case this judgment seems harsh, Andrew Pettegree, on of my favorite historians, managed in only 338 lively pages to describe how Martin Luther, one of the most prolific authors of the 16th century, became “the world’s first mass-media figure, its first brand,” as well as providing a concise biography.

You may have the impression that I did not like the book. Not so! (dramatic pause to quote Sister Michelle of Derry Girls fame)

To the contrary, I am keenly interested in the history of the book. Thus, I diligently read just about every one of these sometimes obscure references; although I often wondered why. I thought the book contained keen insights as to how the “meanings” of a short work with a fairly discernable agenda can transform over time. On of my favorite passages in the book: “Readers with a taste for irony can only marvel at the sight of godly English radicals demanding and subsequently justifying the execution of Charles I based in part on allegations made by a Scots Catholic protected by a Spanish cardinal and associated with a Spanish ambassador, working with a printer in Brussels closely tied to the Infanta Isabella’s regime.” (533) Well stated, more of this and less endless quoting would have made for a better book.
I also reviewed the relevant sections in Spurs book and found no reference to the “secret history.” Although, Spur agrees with these authors that James I’s and Charles I’s affection for the Duke of Buckingham was a substantial liability. Spur seems focused far more on the role of religion and I am inclined to agree with his perspective. Still, a nice study on the power of the printed word for distortion.

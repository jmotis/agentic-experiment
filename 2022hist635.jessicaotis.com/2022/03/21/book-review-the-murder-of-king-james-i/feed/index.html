Bellany & Cogswell provide a vivid illustration of the power of propaganda, especially in unsettled times — and as our reviewers note, 1620s England was such a time. Because the authors provide such detailed context for the events surrounding and following James’s death, we’re better able to understand how and why George Eglisham’s artful rhetoric in “The Forerunner of Revenge” was so successful. The deliberate use of misinformation and conspiracy theories for political purposes is not a recent phenomenon.

So whose propaganda are we to believe: Buckingham’s and Charles’s, the Parliament-Men who presented the case to the House of Lords for Buckingham’s impeachment, or Eglisham? This book provides the basic material for those who want to argue any side of the case. And that, I think, is the strength of the book. It’s the kind of resource, a case study, that Harvard Business School provides for its students–a detailed background of a particular problem or institution that students are then required to analyze and discuss.

The Thirty Years War was raging on the Continent. Fueled partly by religion and partly by the struggle for power between the Hapsburg and Bourbon dynasties, this was a war that James wanted no part of. Britain was too small, too weak, and too poor to be able to play a decisive role in the conflict. But Prince Charles and the Duke of Buckingham wanted to participate militarily–partly to restore Charles’s sister and her husband to their reign of the Bavarian Palatinate, and partly to promote Protestantism and national glory.
I think the reviewers missed the Spanish connection; the Eglisham pamphlet appears to have been funded by the Hapsburgs. I agree with Matthew that the creation and use of narratives (especially by the Hapsburgs and by Parliament) were more important than the truth or falsity of the narratives. I join the reviewers in their conclusion that the book would have benefited from a more assertive editor.

Bellany and Cogswell patted themselves on the back for their sophistication in focusing on what people believed to be true, rather than what is true. Leaving aside the question of how we might know what “is true,” my response is: noted, but I’m skeptical that modern scholars have all missed the significance of Parliament’s antipathy to Buckingham and Charles, whether or not the antipathy was well-founded. I would have appreciated a chapter that focused on the historiography of, say, the last 50 years.

Despite its length, the authors have a great reflection of what socially happened with James’ death. As you rightly point out, there is not time spent debating if it was murder or not (maybe inferred in the title) but how the death of King James is discussed among the court, nobles, and society at large which spins the issue on its head. This becomes a social and political history from both the top and bottom. I have to agree with you that the direct quotes feel awkward and take up so much space, but I did enjoy them (the small parts I did read).
The last major thing I wanted to mention is the *Length and Extremely Specific* medical commentary from the authors. I personally (with some medical background) loved it and found it interesting. The authors mention how James didn’t trust his caretakers or much medicine but put up with some of the procedures they suggested. I am sorry for anyone with a queasy stomach for the beginning portion (Chapter 2 I think?) detailing what James’ symptomatology was and how it progressed. Overall, I think it is a monster book but worth 80% the read at least!

]]> By: Matthew Inman https://2022hist635.jessicaotis.com/2022/03/21/book-review-the-murder-of-king-james-i/#comment-60 Wed, 23 Mar 2022 18:08:12 +0000 https://2022hist635.jessicaotis.com/?p=589#comment-60 Tyler – you’ve done a good job of condensing a very long book that is jammed full with information into a brief review, something that’s not particularly easy to do. You included a fantastic summarizing phrase in your opening, writing that the book addresses the “movement of the radical theory” of James’ murder. I think this really gets to the crux of what Bellany and Cogswell were doing in this book. It was not the facts about James’ death that mattered, but rather what people believed about his manner of death. Beliefs (or perhaps useful opportunities to use rumors for gain by shrewd politicians) are what drove the events of this book. The rumor about James being murdered seeped into multiple arenas, and even though there was no evidence to support the rumor, the belief in it translated into practical, real-world effects.

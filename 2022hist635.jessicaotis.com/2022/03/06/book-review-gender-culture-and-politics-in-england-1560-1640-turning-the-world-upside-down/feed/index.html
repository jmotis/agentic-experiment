I found this work to be incredibly similar to, if not exactly the same as “Good Wives, Nasty Wenches, and anxious Patriarchs” by Kathleen Brown. Both works directly deal with social and gender norms in English-structured societies despite Brown’s work being focused on colonial Virginia. Both works peel back the perception of women as wicked temptresses or pious damsels during this period to reveal the origins of such extremes. They also examine the use of clothing in expression of gender, which I enjoyed immensely.

Amussen and Underdown’s examination of theatre was enlightening. I honestly did not think of festivals and theatre as anything more than entertainment for this era but this work has provided an entirely new lens. I knew of the placement of underdogs into positions of mock power during these cultural practices but I was not aware as to why these traditions were in place.

]]> By: William/Bill https://2022hist635.jessicaotis.com/2022/03/06/book-review-gender-culture-and-politics-in-england-1560-1640-turning-the-world-upside-down/#comment-55 Wed, 09 Mar 2022 23:54:36 +0000 https://2022hist635.jessicaotis.com/?p=565#comment-55 This is a fascinating book. It provides a “view from the bottom” which illustrates the central theme of Underdown’s concept. The 17C in England was indeed a world turned upside down: plague, regicide, Civil (religious) War, Dissenters, revolution, masterless men, population growth. and much more. The social upheaval had been building even before the Tudor era but seemed to accelarate during the late Elizabethan period and reached crisis proportions under the Stuarts. Traditional roles and institutions were, indeed, “turned upside down.” The hierarchical agricultural society of the Middle Ages with its age-old and slowly changing traditions of landholding and fealty, all embedded in a seemingly timeless religious web of meaning and ritual, was changing quickly.

Anna’s observation that the bottoms-up approach can make it hard to see the larger trends. And that really is the only major weakness I see in the book.

Interestingly, one of Prof Underdown’s colleagues, Christopher Hill, wrote a bottoms-up history, The World Turned Upside Down, in 1972. In his introduction Hill noted that his book was diametrically opposed to Underdown’s 1971 Pride’s Purge.

“This book deals with what from one point of view are subsidiary
episodes and ideas in the English Revolution, the
attempts of various groups of the common people to impose
their own solutions to the problems of their time, in opposition
to the wishes of their betters who had called them into political
action. The reader who wishes to restore his perspective might
with advantage read the valuable book recently published by
Professor David Underdown: Pride’s Purge (Oxford U.P.,
1971). This deals with almost exactly the same period as I do,
but from an entirely different angle. His is the view from the top, from Whitehall, mine the worm’s eye view. His index and mine contain totally different lists of names.”

It is interesting that to some extent the world turned upside down was institutionalized through the May Games, carnival, and other rituals perhaps to serve as a safety valve for release of societal tensions. As shown, at the center of the visual metaphors embodied in the charivari were the assumptions that it was woman’s nature to be “prone to sin and disorder” as with Eve, (5) and the failure of the patriarch was implicated if a woman upset established hierarchies.

She does seem to say that the whole discussion of failed patriarchs was her addition, and I think it really complemented and filled out the discussion of unruly women. They seemed to be two sides of the same coin, and the absence of either one would have lessened the book.
I thought that inversion was well-used as the overall concept under which gender, culture, and politics all intersected in English society. Anna rightly points out these intersections in her review. The book’s examination of the performance of inversion highlights the power of cultural ideas to influence politics and society. The messages conveyed by cultural performances, like theater or civic festivals, were teaching people about social norms and the roles that they were expected to play. Certainly, as we saw in the book, traditional patriarchal gender roles were pushed hard by culture and there was vigorous ridicule and social punishment for those who stepped out of bounds. And yet people, particularly women, still acted with independence and undermined traditional roles and ideas.

This book is certainly a military history. However, instead of a long list of weaponry advancement and innovation, I found the author’s focus on power struggles within the navy’s leadership insightful. They demonstrate that innovation in tactics can result from shifts in more than technology. (A key touchstone in traditional military surveys.) More interestingly, the authors’ approach makes obvious the intertwinement of politics, leadership, and military advancement, which is sometimes missing from traditional histories that can separate political, military, and cultural spheres.

On a separate note, I found your review extremely well written. I am curious as to whether your research about the authors revealed anything about why they co-authored this book. The product is close to both of their research interests.

I agree with your statement that the authors seem to downplay their own conclusion when it comes to the English Civil War. However, I still believe that their book provides a wonderful examination of naval warfare during the war. As they note, the navy has not been thoroughly researched inside to context of the civil war. Their book fills a gap in the historiography and helps to shed light on why the English were able to amass such a considerable naval presence in the 17th century. While it was not the focus of the book, I believe that they are narrowing their audience by not including much about the land battles of the civil war. Individuals that do not know how the English Civil War was fought would be left with more questions than answers after reading this book. Overall, I believe that this book was very well researched and provides an insight into one of the overlooked aspects of the English Civil War.

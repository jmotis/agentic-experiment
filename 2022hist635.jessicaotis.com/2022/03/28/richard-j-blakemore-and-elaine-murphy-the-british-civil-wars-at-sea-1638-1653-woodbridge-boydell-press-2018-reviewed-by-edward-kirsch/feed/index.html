]]> By: Logan Skorupa https://2022hist635.jessicaotis.com/2022/03/28/richard-j-blakemore-and-elaine-murphy-the-british-civil-wars-at-sea-1638-1653-woodbridge-boydell-press-2018-reviewed-by-edward-kirsch/#comment-68 Wed, 30 Mar 2022 20:35:20 +0000 https://2022hist635.jessicaotis.com/?p=598#comment-68 First off, I completely agree that Blakemore and Murphey’s work does a vey good job of detailing and presenting the naval warfare aspect of the English civil war. My only semi-real criticism of the book is that it was a bit of a dry read for me and didn’t seem to ‘get into the heads’ of the figures it mentions, instead focusing on presenting the base historical facts with minimal attention paid to the mentalities or the (admittedly difficult to gauge) reasonings of those involved. My favorite section of the book was oddly enough in the introduction and first chapter where the ‘distinction’ between pirate and mariner was discussed and the “battle at the Downs” that demonstrated the ineffectiveness of the English navy at the time was detailed. I just can’t get over the scene described on page 3 where the Dutch forces paid for repairs of Spanish ships and offered them gunpowder so that the Dutch could fight them and go home sooner.

When looking at the authors arguments on the prevailing narrative of English sea power being more complex than ‘Elizabeth good, Stuart bad’, I agree with the pointes they raise on the differing levels of control over naval matters exercised between the two periods. Similarly, I can’t find any faults with their position that the naval conflicts within and surrounding the Civil War were instrumental in forming the British navy into the dominating force that it became in later decades. Overall, I believe that this book achieves what its authors set out to do and made me interested in learning more about certain aspects of maritime life in early modern Europe.

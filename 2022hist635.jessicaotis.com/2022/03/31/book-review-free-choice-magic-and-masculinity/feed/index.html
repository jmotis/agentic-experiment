I know some of the Royal Society members were interested in alchemy (famously, Newton), so learning that some were interested in “magic” doesn’t seem so surprising.

An interest in alchemy seems more relatable than one in magic. At the time the basic concepts of chemistry weren’t clear. Transformation of a substance clearly happens – but how? Clearly, some forces are unseen – such as gravity and magnetism – so why shouldn’t the transformation of, say, lead into gold be due to some such force.

I’m skeptical that the Royal Society itself provided much institutional support for magic investigations. Hooke and the original Secretary (whose name escapes me) seemed to be mainly interested in empirical investigations. Though, come to think of it, Hooke did propose an action-at-a-distance theory to replace Newton’s ether hypothesis. His theory was concerning planetary motion and, hence, testable though. But, maybe there was more interest in unobservable phenomena than I think. I’m far from an expert on the Royal Society, and even farther on the Masons, so I have some homework to do.

Once again, interesting. And the author’s observation about gendered roles among magic inquirers seems to reinforce the idea that culture permeates just about all human activity. Not an unexpected result, but I didn’t have “17C English magic was gendered” on my “things I know” list until today. Thanks.

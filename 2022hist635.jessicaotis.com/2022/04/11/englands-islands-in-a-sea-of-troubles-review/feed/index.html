Cressy presented the islander experiences as complete subcultures within early modern England, and this could’ve been bolstered with voices from each region. In all honesty I had completely overlooked the smaller islands as part of England and the emerging unified empire. I am ashamed to say I didn’t even know where some of them were or the roles they played in later developments ranging from defensive outposts to political prisons to centers of trade and commerce. I was fascinated to find that each island had developed their own subculture and often vastly differed from the English mainland in language, law adherence, and religious practice. The complexity of these subcultures and their relation to that of the English mainland is thoroughly illustrated in Cressy’s work, and make total sense considering the regions’ Celtic, Viking, Norman, and Saxon heritage.

(I would add maritime and cultural history to the list.) He is apparently renown as a leading social historian; however, in telling his “’island story’” he weaves a story that engages each of these historical approaches and illuminates how “national and insular concerns intermeshed.” (2) His social history background is evident in the types of sources used including legal records and pamphlets. I was particularly fascinated that it could be possible for an entity to be avowedly part of a state, or at least loyal to a state, yet nearly completely free of its laws and the jurisdiction of its courts. (“They were dominions of the crown yet not fully part of the kingdom.” (40)) There are a few quirky historical legacies in the U.S., such as Louisiana rejecting English common law and adhering to a more French Roman law approach. However, the federal court system and federal law applies in full in each U.S. state. The situation of the Channel Islands and the Island of Mann are markedly different and exemplify a degree of “legal pluralism” that seems extreme in the context of emergence of modern states. (59) The reality that “neither common law nor parliamentary statute applied in the Channel Islands or the Isle of Man” is astonishing. Cressy’s point that “English authorities were helpless when officials on the Isle of Man gave shelter to Irish pirates rather, rather than arresting them” shows these differences were more than quaint peculiarities. (90) The Channel islanders did not provide troops, and were exempt from the depredations of impressment, forced loans or Ship Money, and among other privileges had the right of neutrality during times of war. ]]> By: William/Bill https://2022hist635.jessicaotis.com/2022/04/11/englands-islands-in-a-sea-of-troubles-review/#comment-88 Wed, 13 Apr 2022 22:05:37 +0000 https://2022hist635.jessicaotis.com/?p=643#comment-88 As our reviewers note, England’s islands were a varied lot. Through his study of this motley collection of peoples and polities Cressy illustrates the nature and challenges of 17C English governance. The islands serve as exemplars of the fragmented and often fractious society that was England. The Isle of Man’s prickly independence represented one extreme, and Anglesey’s social and political integration with the mainland represented the other. By showing the variety, just among this small group of minor parts of English society, Cressy deftly suggests the variety of the people and institutions that comprise the larger whole.

The money quote, I thought, was on page 26:

“Island inhabitants, with varying resources, needs, and interests, appealed to the Council when it suited them, and otherwise downplayed London’s demands. They seem to have valued the benefits of protection and attachment, so long as their sense of heritage and identity was preserved. For much of the time they preferred to be left alone.”

I suspect these sentiments applied to many, if not most, inhabitants of Great Britain and Ireland at the time. Which raises a methodological question: what is the role of social and cultural historiography in understanding events that are dominated by minority factions, which in this period seem to consist of elites and zealots competing for dominance?

Also, this book and Thornton’s article raised another question: how did authority work in this period? Cressy and Thornton (and many of our earlier books and articles) illustrate the messy and confusing nature of power in 17C England. Authority seems to consist primarily direct coercion, i.e., whoever can raise the most powerful military force. Questions of legitimacy clearly mattered in “normal” times, but this whole post-Elizabethan period–maybe even the whole early-modern period–doesn’t seem to have a “normal.” The struggles for power among kings, nobles, gentry, bishops, clerics, and sectarians certainly left marks on society — and social/cultural history can make those marks visible. But we still need to understand the major change agents, their motives, and their constraints.
